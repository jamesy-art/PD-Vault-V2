She lays out clearly why she chose to close her firm – not because it failed, but because the economics and structure of the model no longer held. But I arrive at a different conclusion. The collective freelance model is often framed as progress. I don’t think it is. It’s primarily a response to financial pressure – a way to distribute risk in an increasingly price-driven market. It’s a step backwards, not forward. It’s also not a new way of working (read my article below). But it’s not the same as the historical patronage model. Quite the opposite. The patron or mécénat tradition was built to protect quality. It created the conditions for focus, continuity and long-term development – allowing artists and creators to pursue their work without constantly adapting to short-term market demands. Freelance networks, on the other hand, tend to fragment both responsibility and craft. They optimize for flexibility and cost-efficiency, but rarely for depth, cohesion or excellence over time. To create truly high-quality work, you need a strong foundation – shared standards, long-term collaboration, and an environment where craft can compound. That doesn’t grow in fragments. At Gärde Design Studio, we’re exploring a different model – one that is actually closer to the historical patronage system. We work in close alignment with entrepreneurs and financial partners, creating a structure where creative work can develop with integrity and long-term direction, without being forced to compromise for short-term survival. Because great creative work is not interchangeable. It creates disproportionate value, often worth billions, and should be structured and supported accordingly. There’s also a craft dimension to this. As one of my typography teachers once said: if we start delivering 80% because that’s what the client pays for, we eventually lose the ability to deliver 100%. Over time, 80% becomes 60%. Craft erodes quietly. So for me, this isn’t about rejecting new ways of working. It’s about being clear on what actually enables quality. Fragmentation may solve for flexibility. But it doesn’t build mastery.
