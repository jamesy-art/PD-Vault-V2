  about

  The Moeli workshop

  I am Sylvie Maillard, and through Atelier Moëli, I develop an artistic
  practice rooted in plants, gesture and matter.


  My work stems from a sensitive connection to the living world: gathered
  plants, observed forms, seasons experienced. I compose with what is there,
  with what grows, transforms, and sometimes disappears.


  At the crossroads of linocut, cyanotype and plant prints, I explore different
  ways of making images, images traversed by light, trace and time.


  The workshop

  Located in Nantes, Atelier Moëli is a space for creation and transmission.


  I develop prints, patterns and pieces resulting from research around plants.
  Some creations are designed to be deployed on supports (textile, decoration),
  others exist as freer forms, close to the printed image or the object.


  In parallel, I offer workshops to share these practices: linocut, cyanotype.

  These are times to experiment, observe, slow down and reconnect with gesture
  as well as with life.


  Atelier Moëli, Atelier Nantes, Sylvie Maillard

  Atelier Moëli, Atelier Nantes, Sylvie Maillard, cyanotype, motifs

  Artistic practice

  My work is part of an approach to artisanal printing, where gesture, time and
  material occupy an essential place.

  I explore different techniques (linocut, cyanotype, plant prints) which I
  create a dialogue between. Each one engages a different relationship with the
  image: digging, revealing, exposing, letting appear.


  Linocut anchors me in a precise and committed exploration of materials, where
  the image is constructed through the process of removal.

  Cyanotype opens up a more sensitive and unpredictable space, where light and
  water fully participate in the process, revealing deep blues and hollow forms.

  Plant prints extend this exploration with a more direct approach, where the
  plant becomes both tool and trace.


  Between control and letting go, I seek to leave room for accident, for
  variations, for what escapes.


  These practices share the same intention: to capture a presence, to keep a
  trace of it, and to let the image evolve.


  Patterns

  The motif remains present in my work, as a way of circulating forms and
  extending my research.


  I compose using drawn, engraved or printed elements, which I then assemble
  into rhythms and repetitions.

  Some motifs are designed for applications in textiles or decoration, others
  exist more independently, closer to printmaking.


  In all cases, I take care to preserve the gestures, irregularities and
  material of the initial work.
