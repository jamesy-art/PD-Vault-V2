  2/15 Susannah Cutler is a textile designer who moonlights as a musician, or
  maybe it’s more accurate to say that she’s a musician who moonlights as a
  textile designer—either way, the Brooklyn-based artist has a nimble handle on
  both of these creative pursuits. “I feel like I couldn’t choose one or the
  other—music or art,” she says. “They both serve as an outlet. I feel like I
  get more excited about making art, but I get more upset if I don’t write a
  song or something—it feels more urgent in a way because it’s definitely more
  of an emotional release than painting something is.” Cutler has been touring
  and performing for the past few years, and her debut album Knock Hard, under
  her moniker Yours Are the Only Ears, came out this past spring. All the while,
  though, she’s been attending the Fashion Institute of Technology, studying
  textile design. Her thesis project from earlier this year has now evolved into
  a DIY dressmaking line, which caught the attention of Café Forgot—they added
  Cutler’s pored-over pieces to their recent Tribeca pop-up, which are also
  available to purchase on her Etsy store. Cutler grew up between Stuyvesant
  Town, Manhattan, and upstate in Margaretville, New York, raised in a family of
  musicians—her father is a blues guitarist and her mother was a country singer.
  They encouraged Cutler musically, but in a classic childlike manner, she
  refused her father’s guitar lessons as a kid. “I didn’t really let him teach
  me that much,” she says. “I taught myself. I always feel like I need to do
  everything myself.” Cutler questions whether this has been a hindrance in some
  ways—it took her years to commit to record her debut album after starting to
  tour, mostly because she didn’t want anyone else having a role in shaping her
  songs—but it’s also precisely this independent streak that’s enabled her to
  put her design knowledge to more practical use. Susannah Cutler in one of her

  4/15 After enrolling at Maryland Institute College of Art for fiber arts,
  Cutler realized that she wasn’t a great fit for its traditional fine art
  curriculum. “I really didn’t like having to have a concept about my art,” she
  says. “Contemporary art now is just different than what I was interested in,
  which was something that was tactile and utilitarian, that had a purpose, and
  that was more from a craft viewpoint instead of a fine art viewpoint.” Because
  of this training, though, Cutler became skilled at drawing things from life
  for hours on end, so she figured textile design would more closely align with
  her interests. “I wanted to feel like I was making something that I could use
  in a way that was more enticing for me than just making a sculpture,” she
  says, noting she has an appreciation for laborious processes that aren’t
  immediately satisfying. “I want to work at something. I have this weird thing
  with work, which is why I got into embroidery for a while, because I was like,
  ‘This takes hours—I’m so into it.’ ” She’s also always been interested in the
  historical significations of craftwork such as sewing and embroidery. “I like
  that now it’s my choice. I feel like it’s kind of interesting to reappropriate
  that concept of [crafting], being this thing for weak women to do alone in
  their house, because now I feel like it empowers me.” Weighing all of these
  inclinations, she decided to enroll in FIT’s textile design program last year.
  The curriculum focused primarily on design (aside from one sewing class), so
  Cutler had to watch YouTube tutorials to learn how to sew. Given her desire
  for putting her artistic leanings to practical use, Cutler decided to make her
  own dresses from the textiles that she had been designing in school, all of
  which she exhibited as her capstone thesis project. “A lot of people, when
  they came [to the thesis show], were like, ‘Oh, I’d really like to wear one of
  these,’ which I wasn’t expecting at all. I was like, Whoa, cool, maybe I’ll

  6/15 Cutler’s textiles are inspired by nature, and primarily feature animals,
  flowers, and human figures. Her thesis collection, which also serve as the raw
  fabric for her Café Forgot dresses, are composed of watercolor paintings of
  flowers in a variety of muted colors, whether they’re pale yellow daisies,
  periwinkle lupines, or pink roses, interspersed with naked bodies in stretched
  out poses, or sometimes overlaid on geometric grids. She came up with the
  silhouettes of the garments based on clothes that she would want to wear
  herself. “I feel like all I know how to do is reflect what I would buy, what I
  would want to wear,” Cutler says. “I like comfortable clothes that I don’t
  have to fuss with, that kind of look good no matter what—that are comfortable,
  easy, and kind of effortless.” The dresses, which are appropriately breezy,
  toe the line between wearable art and fashion, which made them a perfect fit
  for Café Forgot’s eclectic collection. “I’m so intuitive in my process that
  it’s really hard for me to justify my art,” Cutler says. “There is something
  in me that’s being voiced, but I don’t necessarily know how to articulate it.
  But I believe it’s there, and I liked making these dresses and feeling like I
  made something from start to finish that has purpose in this world, that
  people can wear—I wanted people to feel like they can do anything in them.”
  Cutler doesn’t quite know what will come of this small-scale, DIY project—she
  hopes one day to be able to do it full-time—but for now, she’s expanding her
  repertoire. She excitedly tells me that she’s learning how to make crop tops
  and skirts: “The crop tops have a little bit of beading, and they’re made from
  twill cotton. It’s a bit heavier than lightweight cotton. It’s olive green.”
  In addition to these new silhouettes, she’s been busy raising her two newly
  adopted kittens (named Bow and Arrow), and likely planning a move upstate
  sometime within the next year. “My dream is to live upstate with my cats,
